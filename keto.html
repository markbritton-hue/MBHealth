Tests You Should Request
Critical markers not in your current panels — recommended by Bikman & Berry
Priority 1
Fasting Insulin
µIU/mL
Bikman optimal: <6  ·  Berry optimal: <5
THE most important missing test. Bikman: "Fasting insulin detects insulin resistance a decade or more before glucose changes." Without it, HOMA-IR cannot be calculated.
Priority 1
hsCRP
mg/L (high-sensitivity C-Reactive Protein)
Optimal: <1.0  ·  Elevated risk: >3.0
Berry's top inflammation marker. Cardiovascular disease is largely an inflammatory process. With elevated glucose and elevated LDL, your hsCRP could be a critical piece of the picture.
Priority 2
Uric Acid
mg/dL
Bikman optimal: <5.5  ·  Standard: <7.0
Bikman lists uric acid as one of his three core heart disease markers. Elevated uric acid correlates with insulin resistance and fructose metabolism.
Priority 2
Vitamin D (25-OH)
ng/mL
Berry optimal: 60–80  ·  Standard min: >30
Berry calls Vitamin D deficiency epidemic. Low Vitamin D correlates with insulin resistance, poor immune function, and cardiovascular risk.
Priority 2
Homocysteine
µmol/L
Optimal: <7  ·  Elevated: >10  ·  High risk: >15
Berry: "Homocysteine is a far better cardiovascular risk marker than LDL." Elevated homocysteine directly damages arterial walls.
Priority 2
Free T3 & Free T4
pg/mL & ng/dL
TSH alone is not sufficient per Berry
Berry: "TSH is a pituitary hormone — it tells you what the brain is asking for, not what the thyroid is delivering." Hypothyroidism worsens insulin resistance and cardiovascular risk.
Priority 3
Ferritin
ng/mL
Optimal: 50–150 (men)  ·  High: >300
Berry: Ferritin is both an iron storage and inflammation marker. Elevated ferritin can indicate chronic inflammation even when CRP appears normal.
Priority 3
ApoA1
mg/dL
You have ApoB (108) — ApoA1 completes the ratio
You already have ApoB tested. Adding ApoA1 gives the ApoB/ApoA1 ratio — one of the strongest cardiovascular risk predictors available. ApoB/ApoA1 <0.9 is optimal.
Priority 3
C-Peptide
ng/mL
Optimal: 0.8–1.8  ·  High indicates IR
Bikman and Berry both recommend C-peptide as a measure of insulin secretion by the pancreas. Shows how hard your pancreas is working to control blood sugar.
